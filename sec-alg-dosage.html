    Section 1.3 Focus on Dosage Calculations

    Nurses, pharmacists, and other healthcare providers must be proficient in calculating dosages of medications to ensure that patients receive a safe but effective dose. In this section, we address the proportional reasoning involved in these calculations.

    Objectives

    Students will be able to:
    • Calculate dosages of medications.
    • Determine if dosages meet safety guidelines.

    Subsection 1.3.1 Calculating Dosages

    In calculating dosages, we will use the approach of multiplying by a conversion factor, or a "fancy 1".

    Example 1.3.2.

    The provider ordered 8,000 units of heparin. The vial is labeled 10,000 units/ml. What volume will you administer?
    Solution.
    We need to convert 8,000 units into mililiters to determine the volume of medication to be administered to the patient. Our conversion factor is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{10000\text{ units}}{1\text{ ml}}=\frac{1\text{ ml}}{10000\text{ units}}=1 \end{equation*}
    We will use the form with units in the denominator so that the units divide out as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{8000\cancel{\text{ units}}}{1}\cdot\frac{1\text{ ml}}{10000\cancel{\text{ units}}}=0.8\text{ ml} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, 0.8 ml of heparin should be administered to the patient.
    Many prescriptions use latin abbreviations to indicate how often the medication should be administered. Some of the more common abbreviations are listed below.

    Dosage Abbreviations.

    bid
    Twice per day (Latin: bis in die)
    tid
    Three times per day (Latin: ter in die)
    qid
    Four times per day (Latin: quarter in die)
    qd
    Every day (Latin: quaqua die); may also be written as od for once daily
    q3h
    Every three hours (Latin: quaqua 3 hora); can generalize to any number of hours
    Additionally, dosage calculations may require converting units. Some of the more commonly used conversions are listed below.

    Unit Conversions.

    \begin{equation*} 1\text{ kilogram (kg)}\approx2.2\text{ pounds (lb)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 1 \text{ gram (g)}=1\,000\text{ miligrams (mg)}=1\,000\,000\text{ micrograms (mcg)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 1\text{ mg}=1\,000\text{ mcg} \end{equation*}
    Note: in many fields, including mathematics, micrograms are denoted as \(\mu\text{g}\text{.}\) To avoid potentially disastrous medical errors caused by confusing micrograms with miligrams, the medical community uses mcg.

    Example 1.3.3.

    The provider orders a maintenance dose of oral aminophylline, 3 mg/kg q6h. The client weighs 110 lbs. How many mg should the nurse administer to the client in a 24-hour period?
    Solution.
    First, we will find the client’s weight in kg:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{110\cancel{ lbs}}{1}\cdot \frac{1\text{ kg}}{2.2\cancel\text{ lb}}=50\text{ kg} \end{equation*}
    Next, we determine the amount of aminophylline administered in each dose:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3\text{ mg}}{1\cancel{\text{ kg}}}\cdot\frac{50\cancel{\text{ kg}}}{1}=150\text{ mg} \end{equation*}
    Since the dose is given every 6 hours, there will be \(\frac{24}{6}=4\) doses in each 24-hour period. This amounts to \(4\cdot150=600\text{ mg}\) each day.
